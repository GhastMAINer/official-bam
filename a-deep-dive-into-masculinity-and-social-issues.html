      “A Deep Dive into Masculinity and Social Issues”

      Short Film Title: Boy Talk (2020) - directed by Dary Ow.Authors: Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay

      The short film Boy Talk (2020) by Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay depicts a dialogue betweenan elderly man and a college student on women, culminating in a confrontation of the topic of rape. Thisfilm underscores the entrenched social issue of rape culture, wherein normalised attitudes andmisconceptions regarding gender roles perpetuate victim-blaming and rationalisation of sexual assault.

      Summary

      Rape culture impacts people, families, and society as a whole. It cultivates dread, pain, and self-doubt invictims while empowering abusers. Families frequently contend with the emotional and psychologicalrepercussions following a loved one's experience of sexual violence. Rape culture reinforces societalinequity, enabling the continuation of detrimental gender stereotypes. It also impacts legal and institutionalresponses, hindering survivors' ability to pursue justice and obtain necessary support.Numerous studies and reports underscore the frequency of sexual violence in the Philippines. ThePhilippine Statistics Authority (PSA) reports that one in four Filipino women aged 15-49 has encounteredphysical or sexual violence. Furthermore, data from the Centre for Women’s Resources (CWR) indicatean increasing trend in gender-based violence, intensified by inadequate legal enforcement and culturalnorms that diminish the significance of the issue. The societal stigma associated with rape survivorsdeters them from reporting assaults, leading to underreporting and diminished awareness of the issue.

      Analysis and interpretation

      Biographical ContextIn Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay's Boy Talk, an issue entangled in society is addressed byrape culture. The two writers share strong backgrounds in discussing social issues in their works.Laurente is known to delve particularly into issues of gender, identity, and social structures. Theircollaboration on Boy Talk speaks to their common interest in critiquing how traditional masculine normsencourage toxic behaviors, such as victim-blaming. This short film is part of their larger vision of bringingattention to the issues of gender inequality and tackling societal structures that condone harm.Understanding their backgrounds helps us to realize why this film so unambiguously challenges prevailinggender stereotypes and the normalization of sexual violence in art for social change.

      Linguistic ContextBoy Talk mainly talks about the interplay between an old man and a college student in order to examineand critique toxic masculinity. The conversations are direct and raw, resembling real-world conversationsin which negative attitudes toward gender and women's rights are perpetuated. The language used by theelderly man to casually objectify women and justify sexual violence adds gravitas to the social critiquebeing made by the story. The tension between the generationally contrasting perspectives of the olderman and the evolving views of the college student serves as a dramatic juxtaposition together with thelatter's increasing consciousness. The film explores symbols like the mentor-mentee relationship todemonstrate how entrenched ideas are passed on and reinforced. The other key narrative device thatreinforces the moral message of the film is that the student's gradual move away from seeing the world asjust the old man's language presents it becomes possible through questioning that leads up to aneventual confrontation.

      Sociocultural Context of Boy Talk

      Rape culture and gender-based violence are often forgotten or accepted as normal realities in manysocieties, from which Boy Talk derives its expression. The film critiques how societal expectations andtraditional masculine ideals become lenses through which men view women and relationships. In manysocieties, this type of conversation is common, where distorted concepts of masculinity lead todehumanizing perceptions of women. Such toxic behaviors are not personal beliefs but are an extensionof an overarching cultural system that allows and perpetuates violence. Uncomfortable, as the filmintended, Boy Talk presented dialogues that must be made uncomfortable to spur changes. Thediscussion was brave, an admirable aspect of the film, which, as I reflect, additionally caused me to seehow ingrained these issues already are in today's culture. Two major talking points prompted by thefilm-the challenge of these regressive notions serves as a reminder of change that must continue tohappen in society at large.

      Emphasis

      The critical role of Boy Talk is seen to be specific, shaped by us the viewers, otherwise represented as“U”. The film certainly seeks to make you uncomfortable in its raw and honest portrayal of toxicmasculinity and rape culture. In one scene, the old man nonchalantly tells a college student, "You shouldhave multiple girlfriends... it's what men do," strongly illustrating how societal attitudes normalize suchdestructive behavior toward women. This exchange thrusts the viewer into facing some uncomfortabletruths about how these behaviors are handed down and sustained in communities. By framing suchconversations as common and unquestioned, the film encourages the viewers to reflect on their ownexperiences and social conditioning.The college student's dawning realization of the old man's obtrusive rhetoric as wrong and the convictionof normalized ideas provide another crux to deepen the viewer's understanding of the moral. Thistransition becomes a lens through which the viewer is encouraged to think about his or her own beliefsabout gender, consent, and societal expectations, a process of reflection that is further fortified by theembarrassment that the entire audience is made to feel. The audience's discomfort serves more than anemotional function; it becomes a mode of prompting active contemplation regarding the everyday efectsof normalized rape culture and toxic masculinity on personal and social behavior. Thus, you as U aredirectly pushed to question the complacent acceptance of those ideals and to become active agents inthe confrontation of these realities in day-to-day life.

      Conclusion

      Restated Topic SentenceThe viewer, represented as “U”, becomes crucial in understanding the influence that Boy Talk (2020)exerts on its audience and its subversion of established social norms.

      Boy Talk (2020) mainly addresses the deep topic and societal issue of rape culture through a dialoguebetween an old man and a socially conscious college student. Here, the old man is a metaphor forobsolete toxic masculinity, while the student is one for the possibility of changing societal attitudes. Thefilm focuses on how harmful gender stereotypes are normalized in such a way as to victimize, thusperpetuating sexual violence. By making viewers uncomfortable with raw, unfiltered discussions, the filmurges the audience to scrutinize their notions about gender inequality and take a stand. The shifting of thestudent's perspective illustrates the need to challenge these stereotypical ideas to create a more justsociety. The narrative forces viewers to reflect upon their own role in perpetuating or deconstructing theseissues.

      This film gives us in depth insight into the consequences of societal norms concerning gender andviolence unchecked and how they can maintain a cycle of torment. Toxic masculinity as depicted in BoyTalk that urges individuals to reassess the ideologies of our current society that are dangerously flawed.As individuals, we have to sensitize ourselves and others by talking about issues of respect, equality, andconsent. As a society, there is a need to create spaces where these uncomfortable but crucial dialoguescan thrive, creating awareness and action against gender-based violence. We need the change to beginat each of our doorsteps, confronting these issues, thereby being a catalyst for a cultural shift toward amore accepting, respectful world.

      The social issue of rape culture, which deals with normalizing sexual violence and victim-blaming throughsocietal attitudes and expectations around gender, is highlighted in Boy Talk (2020).

      Effects on us, our family, and to our societyRape culture is planted deep in society in the form of harmful presuppositions associated with consent,gender roles, and respect. Families often brainwash their young members into accepting wrong notions oftoxic masculine behavior or old-fashioned stereotypical gender roles. As for the larger society, it meanshigher incidences of sexual violence and lesser accountability for perpetrators.

      Information/evidence to showRape culture finds refuge in the normalization of sexual violence through the media, victim-blamingnarratives in cases of assault, and the tendency of society to downplay the impact of rape. Studies showhow notions about gender in society shape behaviors, whereby many assault cases are rationalized onthe grounds of gender stereotypes or attitudes implicating or blaming victims for this behavior. Thus, thedialogue in Boy Talk presents an outright articulation of the tragic belief.

      A response to this social issueRape culture must be dealt with because it afects people’s safety, their ability to make choices, and theirwell-being. Societies, by normalizing harmful behavior, not only perpetuate cycles of violence but alsodeny the protection to vulnerable groups. Therefore, by confronting rape culture, we must build healthyrelationships based on respect and ensure justice for those who experience sexual violence.There are many ways to address this issue of “rape culture”, an approach to this includes important topicslike legal changes, education, and community involvement. Sudip Bhattacharya and Amarjeet Singh saythat a strong legal system along with public awareness can help prevent sexual violence, as seen in theirarticle, 'Guardians of Consent: Enhancing Rape Prevention through Social Control Mechanisms'(Bhattacharya & Singh, 2024). Both formal controls (laws and rules) and informal controls (culturalattitudes) need to be strengthened to change how society views gender-based violence. Governmentsshould support growing educational campaigns and programs that challenge traditional views ofmasculinity and sexual consent, especially focusing on changing social attitudes from an early age. In linewith this, Chase Halsne in 'Dismantling Rape Culture: The Need for a Comprehensive EducationalApproach' argues that sex and gender education should be included in school programs to preventpeople from accepting rape culture (Halsne, 2019). If the right kind of education addresses harmful socialnorms, individuals will be able to recognize and reject victim-blaming attitudes, which will help reducesexual violence.

      Furthermore, raising awareness campaigns that will cultivate a culture of respect, consent, and mutualaccountability can support individual action and collective action. Community engagement and peer-ledinitiatives have greatly contributed to cultural change. Bhattacharya and Singh (2024) buttress theargument for public awareness in combating rape myths and stereotypes: "Public campaigns like 'It's OnUs' in the U.S. have highlighted the potential of encouraging individuals to take responsibility inconfronting sexual violence" (Bhattacharya & Singh, 2024). As suggested by Halsne (2019), thecounteraction of rape culture can only be socialized through a shift in the narrative around genderrelations and sexuality. Therefore, individuals must participate in these cultural shifts by challenging therape myths and standing with survivors of sexual violence.
