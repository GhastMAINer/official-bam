      “A Deep Dive into Masculinity and Social Issues”

      Short Film Title: Boy Talk (2020) - directed by Dary Ow.Authors: Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay

      The short film Boy Talk (2020) by Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay depicts a dialogue betweenan elderly man and a college student on women, culminating in a confrontation of the topic of rape. This
      film underscores the entrenched social issue of rape culture, wherein normalised attitudes and
      misconceptions regarding gender roles perpetuate victim-blaming and rationalisation of sexual assault.
      Summary
      Rape culture impacts people, families, and society as a whole. It cultivates dread, pain, and self-doubt in
      victims while empowering abusers. Families frequently contend with the emotional and psychological
      repercussions following a loved one's experience of sexual violence. Rape culture reinforces societal
      inequity, enabling the continuation of detrimental gender stereotypes. It also impacts legal and institutional
      responses, hindering survivors' ability to pursue justice and obtain necessary support.
      Numerous studies and reports underscore the frequency of sexual violence in the Philippines. The
      Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) reports that one in four Filipino women aged 15-49 has encountered
      physical or sexual violence. Furthermore, data from the Centre for Women’s Resources (CWR) indicate
      an increasing trend in gender-based violence, intensified by inadequate legal enforcement and cultural
      norms that diminish the significance of the issue. The societal stigma associated with rape survivors
      deters them from reporting assaults, leading to underreporting and diminished awareness of the issue.
      Analysis and interpretation
      Biographical Context
      In Cyrene Joyce Laurente and Bryan Nonay's Boy Talk, an issue entangled in society is addressed by
      rape culture. The two writers share strong backgrounds in discussing social issues in their works.
      Laurente is known to delve particularly into issues of gender, identity, and social structures. Their
      collaboration on Boy Talk speaks to their common interest in critiquing how traditional masculine norms
      encourage toxic behaviors, such as victim-blaming. This short film is part of their larger vision of bringing
      attention to the issues of gender inequality and tackling societal structures that condone harm.
      Understanding their backgrounds helps us to realize why this film so unambiguously challenges prevailing
      gender stereotypes and the normalization of sexual violence in art for social change.
      Linguistic Context
      Boy Talk mainly talks about the interplay between an old man and a college student in order to examine
      and critique toxic masculinity. The conversations are direct and raw, resembling real-world conversations
      in which negative attitudes toward gender and women's rights are perpetuated. The language used by the
      elderly man to casually objectify women and justify sexual violence adds gravitas to the social critique
      being made by the story. The tension between the generationally contrasting perspectives of the older
      man and the evolving views of the college student serves as a dramatic juxtaposition together with the
      latter's increasing consciousness. The film explores symbols like the mentor-mentee relationship to
      demonstrate how entrenched ideas are passed on and reinforced. The other key narrative device that
      reinforces the moral message of the film is that the student's gradual move away from seeing the world as
      just the old man's language presents it becomes possible through questioning that leads up to an
      eventual confrontation.
      Sociocultural Context of Boy Talk
      Rape culture and gender-based violence are often forgotten or accepted as normal realities in many
      societies, from which Boy Talk derives its expression. The film critiques how societal expectations and
      traditional masculine ideals become lenses through which men view women and relationships. In many
      societies, this type of conversation is common, where distorted concepts of masculinity lead to
      dehumanizing perceptions of women. Such toxic behaviors are not personal beliefs but are an extension
      of an overarching cultural system that allows and perpetuates violence. Uncomfortable, as the film
      intended, Boy Talk presented dialogues that must be made uncomfortable to spur changes. The
      discussion was brave, an admirable aspect of the film, which, as I reflect, additionally caused me to see
      how ingrained these issues already are in today's culture. Two major talking points prompted by the
      film-the challenge of these regressive notions serves as a reminder of change that must continue to
      happen in society at large.
      Emphasis
      The critical role of Boy Talk is seen to be specific, shaped by us the viewers, otherwise represented as
      “U”. The film certainly seeks to make you uncomfortable in its raw and honest portrayal of toxic
      masculinity and rape culture. In one scene, the old man nonchalantly tells a college student, "You should
      have multiple girlfriends... it's what men do," strongly illustrating how societal attitudes normalize such
      destructive behavior toward women. This exchange thrusts the viewer into facing some uncomfortable
      truths about how these behaviors are handed down and sustained in communities. By framing such
      conversations as common and unquestioned, the film encourages the viewers to reflect on their own
      experiences and social conditioning.
      The college student's dawning realization of the old man's obtrusive rhetoric as wrong and the conviction
      of normalized ideas provide another crux to deepen the viewer's understanding of the moral. This
      transition becomes a lens through which the viewer is encouraged to think about his or her own beliefs
      about gender, consent, and societal expectations, a process of reflection that is further fortified by the
      embarrassment that the entire audience is made to feel. The audience's discomfort serves more than an
      emotional function; it becomes a mode of prompting active contemplation regarding the everyday effects
      of normalized rape culture and toxic masculinity on personal and social behavior. Thus, you as U are
      directly pushed to question the complacent acceptance of those ideals and to become active agents in
      the confrontation of these realities in day-to-day life.
      Conclusion
      Restated Topic Sentence
      The viewer, represented as “U”, becomes crucial in understanding the influence that Boy Talk (2020)
      exerts on its audience and its subversion of established social norms.
      Boy Talk (2020) mainly addresses the deep topic and societal issue of rape culture through a dialogue
      between an old man and a socially conscious college student. Here, the old man is a metaphor for
      obsolete toxic masculinity, while the student is one for the possibility of changing societal attitudes. The
      film focuses on how harmful gender stereotypes are normalized in such a way as to victimize, thus
      perpetuating sexual violence. By making viewers uncomfortable with raw, unfiltered discussions, the film
      urges the audience to scrutinize their notions about gender inequality and take a stand. The shifting of the
      student's perspective illustrates the need to challenge these stereotypical ideas to create a more just
      society. The narrative forces viewers to reflect upon their own role in perpetuating or deconstructing these
      issues.
      This film gives us in depth insight into the consequences of societal norms concerning gender and
      violence unchecked and how they can maintain a cycle of torment. Toxic masculinity as depicted in Boy
      Talk that urges individuals to reassess the ideologies of our current society that are dangerously flawed.
      As individuals, we have to sensitize ourselves and others by talking about issues of respect, equality, and
      consent. As a society, there is a need to create spaces where these uncomfortable but crucial dialogues
      can thrive, creating awareness and action against gender-based violence. We need the change to begin
      at each of our doorsteps, confronting these issues, thereby being a catalyst for a cultural shift toward a
      more accepting, respectful world.
      The social issue of rape culture, which deals with normalizing sexual violence and victim-blaming through
      societal attitudes and expectations around gender, is highlighted in Boy Talk (2020).
      Effects on us, our family, and to our society
      Rape culture is planted deep in society in the form of harmful presuppositions associated with consent,
      gender roles, and respect. Families often brainwash their young members into accepting wrong notions of
      toxic masculine behavior or old-fashioned stereotypical gender roles. As for the larger society, it means
      higher incidences of sexual violence and lesser accountability for perpetrators.
      Information/evidence to show
      Rape culture finds refuge in the normalization of sexual violence through the media, victim-blaming
      narratives in cases of assault, and the tendency of society to downplay the impact of rape. Studies show
      how notions about gender in society shape behaviors, whereby many assault cases are rationalized on
      the grounds of gender stereotypes or attitudes implicating or blaming victims for this behavior. Thus, the
      dialogue in Boy Talk presents an outright articulation of the tragic belief.
      A response to this social issue
      Rape culture must be dealt with because it affects people’s safety, their ability to make choices, and their
      well-being. Societies, by normalizing harmful behavior, not only perpetuate cycles of violence but also
      deny the protection to vulnerable groups. Therefore, by confronting rape culture, we must build healthy
      relationships based on respect and ensure justice for those who experience sexual violence.
      There are many ways to address this issue of “rape culture”, an approach to this includes important topics
      like legal changes, education, and community involvement. Sudip Bhattacharya and Amarjeet Singh say
      that a strong legal system along with public awareness can help prevent sexual violence, as seen in their
      article, 'Guardians of Consent: Enhancing Rape Prevention through Social Control Mechanisms'
      (Bhattacharya & Singh, 2024). Both formal controls (laws and rules) and informal controls (cultural
      attitudes) need to be strengthened to change how society views gender-based violence. Governments
      should support growing educational campaigns and programs that challenge traditional views of
      masculinity and sexual consent, especially focusing on changing social attitudes from an early age. In line
      with this, Chase Halsne in 'Dismantling Rape Culture: The Need for a Comprehensive Educational
      Approach' argues that sex and gender education should be included in school programs to prevent
      people from accepting rape culture (Halsne, 2019). If the right kind of education addresses harmful social
      norms, individuals will be able to recognize and reject victim-blaming attitudes, which will help reduce
      sexual violence.
      Furthermore, raising awareness campaigns that will cultivate a culture of respect, consent, and mutual
      accountability can support individual action and collective action. Community engagement and peer-led
      initiatives have greatly contributed to cultural change. Bhattacharya and Singh (2024) buttress the
      argument for public awareness in combating rape myths and stereotypes: "Public campaigns like 'It's On
      Us' in the U.S. have highlighted the potential of encouraging individuals to take responsibility in
      confronting sexual violence" (Bhattacharya & Singh, 2024). As suggested by Halsne (2019), the
      counteraction of rape culture can only be socialized through a shift in the narrative around gender
      relations and sexuality. Therefore, individuals must participate in these cultural shifts by challenging the
      rape myths and standing with survivors of sexual violence. In this way, both society and individuals can
      put an end to these normative behaviors that perpetuate rape culture and work toward creating a safe and
      equitable environment for all.
